# The Mom Test — Fitzpatrick

**Source.** Rob Fitzpatrick, *The Mom Test*. Named for the rule: ask questions
such that even your mom can't lie to you (because you're asking about her life,
not your idea).

---

## Talk about their life, not your idea

**Definition.** Keep the conversation on the customer's actual life, problems,
and past behavior. Never put the idea on the table to be judged — people lie
(kindly) about ideas and tell the truth about their own past.

**When it applies.** Every discovery conversation, pre- and post-idea.

**When it does NOT.** Closing/selling a validated solution to a ready buyer is a
different conversation (commitment, not learning). Don't run discovery on someone
you're closing, or vice versa.

**Decision cue.** If you've described your idea and are now gauging their
reaction, you've left discovery and entered pitch — the data is contaminated.

**Failure it prevents.** Mistaking polite enthusiasm for evidence.

---

## Specifics in the past, not generics/opinions/hypotheticals

**Definition.** Good data = concrete facts about specific past events and actual
behavior. Bad data = generic claims ("I usually…"), opinions ("I think it'd be
great"), and hypotheticals/futures ("I would definitely use…").

**The three deadly types to exclude or rewrite:**
- **Compliments** ("that's a cool idea") — ego noise, zero information.
- **Generics/fluff** ("I always", "I never", "I would") — not a real event.
- **Future promises** ("I'd buy that") — cheap; unspent.

**Decision cue.** For each answer ask: *is this a specific thing that actually
happened?* If not, redirect to "tell me about the last time…".

**Worked micro-example.** "Do you struggle to coordinate your family?" → "yeah,
totally" (generic, useless). Replace with "When did something last slip between
you and your partner? Walk me through that day." (specific, usable).

**Failure it prevents.** Building on imagined behavior instead of real behavior.

---

## Commitment & advancement currencies

**Definition.** A meeting "went well" only if the other person gave up something
they value (**currency**) and moved to a defined next step (**advancement**).

- **Currencies:** **time** (a scheduled working session, doing homework),
  **reputation** (an intro, a public endorsement, bringing a colleague),
  **money** (deposit, pre-order, paid pilot, LOI).
- **Advancement:** a concrete, scheduled next step — not "stay in touch."

**Decision cue.** End every conversation by asking for a realistic commitment.
After it, write down: *currency spent? next step locked?* If both are no, you
have no signal — enthusiasm notwithstanding.

**Worked micro-example.** *vai-nascer-v2*: "Loved it!" = nothing. "Can I get my
partner on a call Thursday and you show us the family flow?" accepted = time +
advancement = real signal.

**Failure it prevents.** Counting compliments and "keep me posted" as validated
demand.

---

## Deflecting compliments

**Definition.** When you get a compliment, don't accept it as data — deflect and
dig: redirect to specifics ("thanks — has that ever actually been a problem for
you? when?"). Compliments most often appear right when you've slipped into
pitching.

**Decision cue.** Compliment received → (1) note you may have pitched, (2)
deflect, (3) ask for the last real instance, (4) probe for a commitment.

**Failure it prevents.** Walking away "validated" on flattery.
