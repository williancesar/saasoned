# The Hook Model — Eyal

**Source.** Nir Eyal, *Hooked*. A habit forms when a user passes through four
stages enough times that the behavior becomes automatic, driven by an internal
trigger.

**Definition.** Trigger → Action → Variable Reward → Investment, looped.

---

## 1. Trigger

**Definition.** What initiates the behavior. **External** triggers are in the
environment (notification, email, app icon, a friend's prompt). **Internal**
triggers are emotions/situations/routines the product becomes coupled with
(boredom, loneliness, uncertainty, "did I do this right?"). Habits exist only
when an *internal* trigger reliably prompts the action without external nudging.

**When it applies.** Any retention design; "users only come when we email them."

**When it does NOT.** Pre-fit products with no value yet — fix value first.

**Decision cue.** Name the specific internal trigger (an emotion + situation). If
you can only name external ones, the loop will churn the moment you stop pushing.

**Failure it prevents.** Notification-spam retention that inflates short-term
metrics and drives uninstalls.

## 2. Action

**Definition.** The simplest behavior done in anticipation of a reward. Governed
by the Fogg Behavior Model (see fogg-behavior-model.md): the action happens only
when motivation, ability, and a trigger coincide.

**Decision cue.** If the action isn't happening, diagnose *which* Fogg factor is
missing and, by default, **increase ability (reduce effort)** before adding
motivation or more triggers.

**Failure it prevents.** Piling on persuasion when the real blocker is friction.

## 3. Variable Reward

**Definition.** The reward that resolves the trigger's need — and it must be
**variable**. Predictable rewards don't sustain habits; variability holds
attention. Three types (see variable-rewards.md): rewards of the **tribe**
(social), the **hunt** (resources/information), the **self** (mastery/
completion).

**Decision cue.** Is the reward variable, and is its *type* matched to the
internal trigger? Predictable or mismatched reward → no habit.

**Failure it prevents.** Static loyalty mechanics that never become habitual.

## 4. Investment

**Definition.** The user puts something in — data, content, followers, effort,
reputation — that (a) loads the *next* trigger, (b) makes the product better for
them over time, and (c) raises the switching cost. Investment is what makes the
loop compound rather than reset.

**Decision cue.** After the reward, what does the user store that makes the next
loop more likely and more valuable? If nothing, the loop doesn't compound.

**Failure it prevents.** A reward-only loop with no stored value, no
next-trigger load, and no moat — easy to leave.

---

## The Habit Zone

**Definition.** A behavior becomes a habit when it has both **sufficient
frequency** and **sufficient perceived utility**. Infrequent behaviors rarely
become habits no matter how useful; frequent-but-useless ones don't either.

**Decision cue.** Plot the target behavior on frequency × perceived utility. Low
frequency → either find a higher-frequency hook, or accept this is not a
habit-formation play (retain via other means). Don't force a habit onto an
inherently rare behavior.

**Worked micro-example.** *vai-nascer-v2* "weekly symptom check": frequency
weekly (borderline) but perceived utility high during pregnancy → habit-viable
for the pregnancy window specifically; don't expect it to persist post-birth
without a new hook.

**Failure it prevents.** Spending habit-design effort on inherently
low-frequency behaviors that can't become habits.
