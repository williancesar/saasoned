# Crossing the Chasm — Moore

**Source.** Geoffrey Moore, *Crossing the Chasm*.

---

## Technology Adoption Life Cycle (TALC)

**Definition.** Adopters split into innovators → early adopters (visionaries) →
early majority (pragmatists) → late majority (conservatives) → laggards. The
groups buy for *different reasons*; messaging that wins one can repel the next.

**When it applies.** Any "we have some keen users but growth stalled"; planning
the move from first fans to mainstream.

**When it does NOT.** Pre-product-fit (no adopters yet) — that's discovery.

**Decision cue.** Identify which group your current users are. Early-adopter
traction does **not** predict early-majority traction; assume a gap.

---

## The chasm

**Definition.** A deep gap between early adopters (buy vision, tolerate
roughness) and the early majority (buy proven, whole solutions with references).
Products that delight early adopters routinely die in this gap.

**Decision cue.** If users are visionaries and you're targeting "everyone next,"
you are about to fall in. Don't broaden — pick a beachhead.

**Failure it prevents.** Mistaking early-adopter love for mainstream readiness
and scaling into the chasm.

---

## Beachhead and the bowling alley

**Definition.** **Beachhead** = the single, narrow early-majority segment you
attack first, chosen because you can dominate it completely. **Bowling alley** =
adjacent segments you topple next, each made easier by dominating the prior one.

**When it applies.** Crossing the chasm; choosing the first mainstream segment.

**When it does NOT.** Before you have a repeatable value for *any* segment.

**Decision cue.** Pick the segment where: pain is urgent, the segment talks to
itself (references travel), and your whole product can be completed for it with
current resources. Smaller-but-dominable beats larger-but-contested.

**Worked micro-example.** *vai-nascer-v2*: beachhead = first-trimester pregnant
women in Brazil who want family involved — not "expectant parents globally."
Win that, then bowl into adjacent trimesters/segments.

**Failure it prevents.** Spreading thin across many segments and dominating
none — stalling forever in the chasm.

---

## Whole product

**Definition.** The early majority adopts the *whole* product — core plus
everything required to have a compelling reason to buy (integrations, support,
onboarding, proof, ecosystem) — not just the core technology.

**Decision cue.** For the beachhead, list the gap between "what we ship" and
"what they need to fully adopt." Fill that gap (build, partner, or service it)
before scaling acquisition.

**Failure it prevents.** Scaling a core product the mainstream can't actually
adopt because the surrounding necessities are missing.

---

## D-Day strategy

**Definition.** Concentrate overwhelming force on one beachhead segment to win
it decisively and fast — a deliberate over-investment in a narrow target rather
than thin presence everywhere.

**Decision cue.** Effort going to >1 segment pre-chasm-crossing → re-concentrate
on the single beachhead.

**Failure it prevents.** Diffuse effort that never achieves dominance anywhere.

---

## The elevator-pitch / positioning-statement formula

**Definition.** Moore's fill-in template:

> **For** [target customer]
> **who** [statement of need or opportunity],
> **the** [product name]
> **is a** [market category]
> **that** [key benefit / compelling reason to buy].
> **Unlike** [primary competitive alternative],
> **our product** [primary differentiation].

**Decision cue.** Every blank must trace to a Dunford component (target ←
best-fit; category ← component 5; differentiation ← unique attributes;
alternative ← real competitive alternative). A blank you can't fill from
evidence is unfinished positioning work, not a copywriting problem.

**Worked micro-example.** *vai-nascer-v2*: "**For** first-trimester pregnant
women in Brazil **who** want their partner and family involved without each
managing a separate app, **vai-nascer** **is a** shared pregnancy companion
**that** keeps the whole family synced on one subscription. **Unlike** generic
week-by-week trackers and group chats, **our product** ties family access to the
mother's plan so involvement is one decision, not many."

**Failure it prevents.** A pitch that lists features but never states for-whom,
what-category, and versus-what — leaving the listener unable to place the value.
