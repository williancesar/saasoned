# Obviously Awesome — Dunford

**Source.** April Dunford, *Obviously Awesome*. Core claim: **positioning is
context you choose deliberately**, not an immutable property of the product. The
same product positioned in a different market frame can go from confusing to
obviously valuable.

---

## The 5(+1) components

**Definition.** Positioning is fully specified by five components (plus an
optional sixth):

1. **Competitive alternatives.** What the customer would *actually* do if your
   product didn't exist (often: a spreadsheet, a manual habit, a generalist
   tool, or nothing — frequently not a direct competitor).
2. **Unique attributes.** Capabilities/features you have that the alternatives
   do not.
3. **Value (and proof).** The benefit those unique attributes enable, plus
   evidence it's real. Attributes without mapped value are noise.
4. **Best-fit customer characteristics.** The identifiable traits of customers
   who care *a lot* about that value (so you can find more of them).
5. **Market category.** The frame you put the product in so the value is
   immediately obvious to the best-fit customer.
6. *(+1, optional)* **Relevant trend.** A wave the customer already cares about,
   layered on only if genuine — never a substitute for components 1–5.

**When it applies.** Any vague/confusing value prop; any "who is this for";
before writing landing-page or pitch copy.

**When it does NOT.** When you don't yet know who gets value (no "lovers"
identified) — do `growth-customer-discovery` first; you can't fake component 4.

**Decision cue.** Walk components in order. The first one you can't answer with
evidence is the work to do next. If component 5 (category) is wrong, no amount
of components 2–3 polish fixes "nobody gets it."

**Worked micro-example.** *family-agent*: alternatives = shared calendar +
group chat + paper chart; unique attribute = an agent that proactively
reconciles family logistics; value = less coordination overhead, fewer dropped
balls; proof = time saved in a pilot; best-fit = dual-income households with
school-age kids already failing at a shared calendar; category framed as
"family operations assistant," not "another calendar."

**Failure it prevents.** Feature-list marketing that never tells the customer
what the product is *for* or why it beats what they do today.

## The 10-step process

**Definition.** Dunford's sequence to derive positioning deliberately:

1. Understand the customers who already love the product.
2. Form a cross-functional positioning team.
3. Align on positioning vocabulary; let go of legacy/aspirational baggage.
4. List true competitive alternatives (what they'd really use otherwise).
5. Isolate unique attributes vs. those alternatives.
6. Map attributes → the value they enable (with proof).
7. Determine the best-fit customers who care most about that value.
8. Find the market category/frame that makes the strengths obvious.
9. *(Optional)* layer on a relevant trend.
10. Capture positioning in a shared document and align the org.

**Decision cue.** Don't start at step 8 (category) — teams love to argue
category first. Category is an *output* of steps 4–7, not an opinion.

**Failure it prevents.** Positioning-by-committee opinion instead of
positioning derived from who actually gets value.

---

## Positioning styles

**Definition.** Three deliberate stances for the category frame:

- **Head-to-head** — compete to win an existing category outright. Use when you
  can credibly claim leadership of a category customers already understand.
- **Big fish, small pond** — own a *subsegment* of an existing category. Use
  when the broad category is owned but a defensible niche is open (the common
  startup choice; aligns with niche-monopoly).
- **Create a new category** — define a new frame. Highest cost/risk (you must
  teach the market the category exists and matters); only when no existing
  frame fits and you can fund the education.

**Decision cue.** Default to **big fish, small pond** unless you can fund
category creation or genuinely lead an existing category. This is the
positioning expression of the hub's niche-monopoly stance.

**Failure it prevents.** Picking the glamorous "new category" play without the
budget to teach the market, or fighting head-to-head in a category you can't
lead.
