# Growth: Positioning

Owns "what is this, for whom, in what context." Hub: `growth-foundations`
(worldview, shared vocabulary, router). Does not redefine hub terms.

**Stance.** Positioning is a deliberate choice, not destiny (Dunford). Pick
**one beachhead** small enough to dominate (Moore + *Zero to One*) before any
broad-market language. "It's for everyone" is the failure, not the safe option.

## The positioning-rewrite procedure (default deliverable)

When asked to fix a vague value proposition, run this and output both artifacts:

1. **Find the lovers.** Who already gets disproportionate value? (input to
   best-fit customers; if unknown → `growth-customer-discovery` first.)
2. **List true competitive alternatives** — what the customer would *actually*
   do without you (often a spreadsheet, a habit, or nothing — not a rival app).
3. **Isolate unique attributes** alternatives lack.
4. **Map each attribute → value → proof.**
5. **Name the best-fit customer characteristics** that predict who cares most.
6. **Choose the market category** that makes the value obvious (the frame).
7. *(Optional)* layer a **trend** only if it's real and the customer cares.
8. **Pick the beachhead** (Moore): the single segment to dominate first.
9. **Emit the Moore elevator pitch** (template in crossing-the-chasm.md).
10. **State the whole product** the beachhead needs to fully adopt.

Full worked walkthrough: [references/positioning-rewrite.md](references/positioning-rewrite.md).

## Decision cues

- **"Nobody understands what we do"** → wrong market category (component 5);
  re-frame, don't add features.
- **"How do we beat [competitor]?"** → first verify it's a real competitive
  alternative; often the true alternative is "do nothing / use a spreadsheet."
- **"Who's our market?"** → beachhead selection: smallest segment you can
  dominate, not the largest you could plausibly serve.
- **"Should we say it's for everyone?"** → **No.** Niche-monopoly first
  (hub Guardrail 5); narrow until you can be the obvious first choice.
- **Stuck between early adopters and mainstream** → the chasm; pick one
  beachhead and concentrate (D-Day).

## When this skill does NOT apply

- "Which channel reaches that segment" → `growth-traction-channels`.
- "Do these customers/problem even exist" → `growth-customer-discovery`.
- "How do we measure if the new positioning worked" → `growth-experiments`.

## Anti-patterns (this skill's catalog)

1. **Broad-market positioning before niche domination** (hub anti-pattern 5).
2. **Inventing a competitor** instead of naming the real alternative (status
   quo, spreadsheet, nothing) — leads to false differentiation.
3. **Feature list as positioning** — attributes with no mapped value/proof.
4. **Wrong category frame** — describing a new thing as a familiar category that
   sets the wrong expectations (or a novel category nobody searches for).
5. **Positioning before knowing who loves it** — guessing best-fit customers
   instead of learning them (route to discovery).
6. **Treating positioning as permanent** — never revisiting after the market or
   the lovers change.

## Worked example (illustrative — not a product spec)

*vai-nascer-v2*: "a pregnancy app" is a category, not a position. Rewrite:
competitive alternative = generic week-by-week trackers + group chats; unique
attribute = family access tied to the pregnant woman's plan; value = the partner
and family stay synced without each buying in; best-fit = first-trimester
pregnant women in Brazil who want family involved. Beachhead = exactly that
segment. The elevator pitch and whole product follow from there
(see positioning-rewrite.md).
